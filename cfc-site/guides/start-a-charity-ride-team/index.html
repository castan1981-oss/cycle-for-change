How to start a charity ride team

Teams finish at higher rates, raise more per rider, and have more fun doing it. You need a name, three committed people, and a group chat. The rest compounds.

Solo charity riding works. But teams are the cheat code: shared training rides, shared fundraising momentum, and someone to hold the pace when you crack at mile 60. Most big rides are built around team culture — the jerseys, the tents, the leaderboards.

Recruiting: three is a team

Don't wait for ten people. Get two friends to register and you're real. Announce the team where your people already are, and make the ask about the experience — "train with me all spring, do something huge in the fall" — not about obligation.

The mechanics

Most ride platforms let you form a named team; individual minimums usually still apply, but team totals unlock perks — and cover riders who fall short. Set a team goal 20% above the sum of minimums so nobody sweats alone.

Keep them in

The dropout window is weeks 4–8, when the novelty is gone and the long rides aren't yet. One standing weekend ride, one group chat, one monthly milestone celebration. Attendance beats intensity — the team that rides together at 14 mph finishes together.

Captains carry the fundraising energy too: share the fundraising playbook in week one, and celebrate every donor publicly.
