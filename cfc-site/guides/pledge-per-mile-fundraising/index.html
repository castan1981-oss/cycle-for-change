Pledge-per-mile fundraising, explained

Instead of asking for a number, you ask for a rate: a few cents for every mile you ride. The donation grows with the effort — and every training ride becomes part of the campaign.

Standard charity fundraising asks for a fixed gift. Pledge-per-mile flips it: a donor commits to, say, five cents a mile, and the total follows the riding. 1,000 miles later, that's $50 — earned a mile at a time, in public.

Why it works

It ties the money to the effort. A donor who pledges per mile checks your tally. They're invested in your Tuesday ride. The relationship inverts — instead of you chasing donors, your donors start chasing your miles.

It also lowers the barrier. "Two cents a mile" sounds like nothing; across a 7,500-mile year it's $150. People say yes to rates who'd hesitate at totals.

How to run it

You need three things: a public, trustworthy mileage count (a live tally fed by your ride tracker — screenshots don't cut it); a clear cap or settlement date so pledgers know when they give; and radical clarity about where money goes.

That's exactly how this site is built: the tally updates from Strava, pledges are free to make with no card required, and at year-end pledgers vote on which queer-community orgs receive the money — then give directly. No middleman, no cut.

A pledge is a bet on somebody finishing. That's a better relationship than a receipt.

Want to see it from the pledger side? Get on the board — it takes a name and nothing else.
