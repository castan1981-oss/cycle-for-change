Is Cycling Good for Anxiety?

Cycling won't cure anxiety. But it reliably interrupts it. An hour on the bike puts a hard break in the loop — the kind most other things can't manage. That's not a small thing.

Is cycling good for anxiety — what the research shows

The anxious brain runs the same track on repeat. A conversation from three days ago. Something you said, something someone said to you. It doesn't stop on its own.

What the bike does is give your body something loud enough to compete with it. Not to silence the noise — to redirect your attention somewhere concrete: the road, the next climb, your breath.

The research on aerobic exercise and anxiety is consistent. Regular cardio reduces symptoms. The mechanism isn't fully mapped — endorphins are part of it, but so is the break from rumination, the sense of accomplishment, and the shift in nervous-system state that comes from sustained physical effort. Studies have placed aerobic exercise alongside other established interventions for mild to moderate anxiety, not as a replacement for treatment, but as a legitimate, low-risk tool.

I'm not a clinician. What I know is what happens when I ride.

The first fifteen minutes are usually rough

If anxiety is running high when you clip in, the early stretch can feel worse. Tight chest. Restless legs. The urge to stop and go back inside.

Stay on.

Around mile five, something shifts. The breath evens out. The body takes over. You stop narrating the inside of your head and start actually riding.

That transition — from noise to motion — is the whole point. It doesn't happen every single time. Some rides are hard the whole way through. But the consistency over weeks and months adds up. Anxiety doesn't disappear. Its volume comes down.

The bike doesn't fix anything. It creates space. Sometimes space is what you need to fix it yourself.

A note on queer anxiety specifically

Anxiety looks different when you're queer. It's often layered — a baseline, plus the particular vigilance that comes from navigating spaces that weren't built for you. That's real, and it accumulates over time.

I'm not logging 7,500 miles because riding is a cure. I'm doing it because movement has helped me manage what chronic stress does to a body, and because every mile I log raises money for organizations doing direct work on the systemic causes of that stress — the LA LGBT Center, the SF AIDS Foundation, one·n·ten.
