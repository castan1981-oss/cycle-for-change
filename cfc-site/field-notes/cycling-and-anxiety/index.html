Is Cycling Good for Anxiety?

Yes. Not because of the endorphins — though those are real. A long ride changes the quality of your attention in a way that's hard to replicate. Here's what I've noticed, riding with anxiety for years.

What cycling does for anxiety, from the saddle

The first ten miles are usually the hardest. Whatever I brought from the morning — the loop of thought, the low hum of dread that doesn't have a name — it's still there. I'm not riding away from it. It rides with me.

Then something shifts. Not dramatically. Around mile twelve or fifteen, the road takes enough of my attention that the loop quiets. I'm watching the shoulder. I'm tracking my cadence. I'm reading the wind. The part of my brain that runs on catastrophe doesn't disappear — it just runs out of bandwidth.

That's the honest version. Cycling doesn't fix anxiety. It doesn't cure it. What it does is interrupt the pattern long enough that you come back from the ride different than you left.

Is cycling good for anxiety? What research suggests

The short answer the research gives: yes. Aerobic exercise is consistently associated with reduced anxiety symptoms across a wide body of studies. Movement regulates the nervous system over time. It shifts mood. A long, steady-effort ride tends to do more for anxiety than a short burst — something about sustaining the effort past the point where the noise runs out.

I'm not a clinician. This isn't medical advice. What I can offer is experience: years of riding with anxiety, and a consistent pattern of getting off the bike different than I got on.

The research also suggests duration matters more than intensity for this. A long moderate ride often does more than a short hard one. That tracks with what I feel. The first thirty minutes are noise. The second thirty minutes are where it gets quiet.

Distraction versus something that stays

There's a version of riding that's just avoidance. Get on the bike so you don't have to sit with anything. That's not what I'm talking about.

The kind of ride I mean is long enough that you can't avoid yourself. You hit the wall where the distraction runs out. You're left with whatever you brought. That's where it gets real — not in the legs, but in deciding to keep going anyway.

I've found that coming out the other side of that wall is what stays with you. Not the endorphins after. The fact that you stayed.

The ride doesn't fix what's hard. It proves you can keep moving while it's still hard.

Why I keep going — and what the miles are for

I'm in recovery. I've lived with anxiety most of my adult life. I know what it costs. I also know that getting on the bike has been, over and over, one of the things that makes it livable.

Queer people carry disproportionate rates of anxiety, depression, and chronic stress. That's documented. The organizations this pledge supports work in that space every day — in Phoenix, in LA, across the country. Every mile I log goes toward funding them, by vote, at the end of the year.

That's why the pledge exists. Not to prove anything. To do something real, with a body that keeps showing up, for communities that need it.
